Citing Gandalf, Pope Leo says we must "disarm" AI
With the co-founder of Anthropic at his side today in Rome, Pope Leo XIV released a major new encyclical —his first—called “Magnifica Humanitas” (“Magnificent Humanity”). It calls for AI to be “disarmed” in service of the common good. “The word is strong,” Leo admits, but he chose the language of “disarmament” deliberately “because this moment needs words capable of attracting attention, awakening consciences, and indicating paths forward for humanity.” AI today must be “freed from logics that turn it into an instrument of domination, exclusion, and death.” The 40,000-word encyclical contains uncompromising critiques of AI-powered autonomous weapons, neo-colonial attitudes towards data collection, and the hoarding of “new forms of property, such as patents, algorithms, digital platforms, technological infrastructure, and data.” But the letter goes far beyond critique, updating Catholic social teaching in a way that calls on everyone to “build”—a favorite term of the Silicon Valley elite. (See venture capitalist Marc Andreessen’s well-known 2020 essay, “ It’s Time to Build .”) In Leo’s vision, though, this “building” extends beyond code or startups or factories or housing. He calls for nothing less than the creation of a “civilization of love” in which everyone works for the common good within their own sphere of life and in which technology does not dominate, exclude, or bypass humanity, but instead serves and augments it. That is why, despite releasing it today, Leo actually signed the encyclical on May 15, the anniversary of a famous 1891 encyclical called “ Rerum Novarum ” (“New Things”). That older document set out Catholic social teaching during an era of capitalist upheaval, largely taking the side of workers and labor unions. Today, Leo updates the church’s social teaching for the age of AI, which he sees as the “ res novae of our time.” As his predecessor did 135 years ago, Leo warns that individual humans and humanity itself must not be left behind by technological advancements or by new forms of power. He is clear-eyed about the sway that technological elites hold today, comparing them to colonial conquerors. Entire regions, especially those marked by structural fragility and limited geopolitical relevance, are currently subjected to a new mindset of extraction: that of health data, epidemiological profiles, genetic maps and demographic information. These have become the new “rare earths” of power: vital data which, once aggregated and analyzed, can be used to train predictive models, guide investment strategies, anticipate crises and, above all, determine who and what is deemed to matter. Those who control the health data of entire peoples—often collected under the pretext of aid, research or innovation—possess a structural leverage over the future, for they can shape needs and markets. They can also decide, before others, to whom medicines, investments and protections will be allocated. Here lies one of the most urgent moral challenges of our time: to ensure that shared knowledge becomes a true common good rather than an instrument of dominance. If we don’t figure this out, Leo says, “the digital age will not be post-colonial, but colonial in another form.” Still, the Vatican is not opposed to AI as a tool. Indeed, this spring it rolled out an AI-powered system that will translate services at St. Peter’s into 60 languages on people’s smartphones. But Magnifica Humanitas argues that AI must be kept in perspective, since “these systems merely imitate certain functions of human intelligence.” While they may be faster thinkers, AI tools “do not undergo experiences, do not possess a body, do not feel joy or pain, do not mature through relationships, and do not know from within what love, work, friendship, or responsibility mean. Nor do they have a moral conscience.” That is why, Leo argues, we should not be led astray by AI’s focus on “intelligence.” Elevating one quality of the human person in this way can overshadow “other essential dimensions of life, such as affection, the will, commitment, and relationships.” If you give humans mere technical power apart from wisdom, emotion, and relationships, Leo says, it “does not make us more capable; it makes us more isolated and more vulnerable to being dominated and excluded.” Because of this reality, AI must be “disarmed,” Leo concludes, freeing it “from monopolistic control and opening it to discussion and debate, therefore making it human-friendly and restoring it to the plurality of human cultures and ways of life.” Mere regulation is “insufficient.” The disarmament thus takes place as an ecological project, one that situates AI within the broad sweep of human culture and that orients it towards human flourishing, not toward warfare, monopolistic power, or new inequalities. It calls upon us simultaneously to resist technological domination and to build through “small and steadfast acts of fidelity that serve as a bulwark against dehumanization.” To adopt the mindset needed to build this new “civilization of love,” Leo suggests five pathways that individuals and institutions can each embrace: the need to disarm words, building peace through justice, adopting the perspective of victims, cultivating a healthy realism, and reviving dialogue and multilateralism. In sounding this call to both disarm and to build, Leo turns to “twentieth-century Catholic author” JRR Tolkien. Though he can’t quite bring himself to say that he’s quoting Gandalf from Lord of the Rings , that’s exactly what’s happening.

教皇利奥援引甘道夫的话说，我们必须“解除”人工智能的武装
今天，教皇利奥十四世在罗马与Anthropic的联合创始人一起发布了一份重要的新通谕，这是他的第一份通谕，名为“Magnifica Humanitas”（“壮丽的人性”）。它呼吁人工智能为了共同利益而“解除武装”。“这个词很强大，”Leo承认，但他故意选择了“裁军”一词，“因为这一刻需要能够吸引注意力、唤醒良知和指明人类前进道路的词语。”今天的人工智能必须“摆脱将其变成统治、排斥和死亡工具的逻辑”。这封40000字的通谕对人工智能驱动的自主武器、对数据收集的新殖民主义态度以及囤积“专利、算法、数字平台、技术基础设施和数据等新形式的财产”进行了毫不妥协的批评。但这封信远远超出了批评的范围，以一种呼吁每个人“建设”的方式更新了天主教的社会教学，这是硅谷精英最喜欢的术语。这是因为。（见风险投资家马克·安德森2020年著名的文章《是时候建设了》。）然而，在利奥的愿景中，这种“建设”超越了代码、初创公司、工厂或住房。他呼吁建立一种“爱的文明”，在这种文明中，每个人都在自己的生活领域内为共同利益而努力，技术不会主导、排斥或绕过人类，而是服务和增强人类。这就是为什么，尽管今天发布了通谕，利奥实际上还是在5月15日签署了通谕。这一天是1891年著名通谕《新事物》的周年纪念日。这份旧文件阐述了资本主义动荡时期的天主教社会教义，主要站在工人和工会一边。今天，Leo更新了教会针对人工智能时代的社会教学，他认为这是“我们时代的创新”。正如他的前任135年前所做的那样，Leo警告说，个人和人类本身不能被技术进步或新形式的权力抛在后面。他清楚地看到了当今技术精英的影响力，将他们比作殖民征服者。整个地区，特别是那些以结构脆弱和地缘政治相关性有限为特征的地区，目前都面临着一种新的提取思维：健康数据、流行病学概况、基因图谱和人口信息。这些已成为新的权力“稀土”：重要数据，一旦汇总和分析，可用于训练预测模型、指导投资策略、预测危机，最重要的是，确定谁和什么被认为是重要的。那些控制着全体人民健康数据的人——通常是以援助、研究或创新为借口收集的——对未来具有结构性影响力，因为他们可以塑造需求和市场。他们还可以在其他人之前决定将药物、投资和保护分配给谁。这就是我们这个时代最紧迫的道德挑战之一：确保共享知识成为真正的共同利益，而不是统治的工具。Leo说，如果我们不弄清楚这一点，“数字时代将不会是后殖民时代，而是另一种形式的殖民时代。”尽管如此，梵蒂冈并不反对将人工智能作为一种工具。事实上，今年春天，它推出了一个人工智能驱动的系统，该系统将在人们的智能手机上将圣彼得大教堂的服务翻译成60种语言。但Magnifica Humanitas认为，必须正确看待人工智能，因为“这些系统只是模仿人类智能的某些功能。”虽然它们可能思维更快，但人工智能工具“不会经历经验，不会拥有身体，不会感到快乐或痛苦，不会通过关系成熟，也不知道爱、工作、友谊或责任意味着什么。它们也没有道德良知。”Leo认为，这就是为什么我们不应该被人工智能对“智能”的关注所误导。以这种方式提高人的一种品质可能会掩盖“生活的其他基本方面，如感情、意志、承诺和关系”。如果你只给人类技术上的东西。Leo说，除了智慧、情感和人际关系之外，权力“并没有让我们更有能力；它让我们更孤立，更容易被支配和排斥。”Leo总结道，由于这一现实，人工智能必须“解除武装”，将其“从垄断控制中解放出来，开放讨论和辩论，从而使其对人类友好，并将其恢复到多种人类文化和生活方式中。”仅仅监管是“不够的”。因此，裁军是一个生态项目，它将人工智能置于人类文化的广泛范围内，使其面向人类繁荣，而不是战争、垄断权力或新的不平等。它呼吁我们同时抵制技术统治，并通过“小而坚定的忠诚行为来建设，这些行为是防止非人性化的堡垒”。为了采取建立这种新的“爱的文明”所需的心态，利奥提出了个人和机构可以各自接受的五条途径：需要解除言语的武装，通过正义建立和平，采取受害者的观点，培养健康的现实主义，以及恢复对话和多边主义。在发出解除武装和建设的呼吁时，利奥转向了“二十世纪天主教作家”JRR托尔金。虽然他不能完全说他引用了《指环王》中的甘道夫，但这正是正在发生的事情。
