How MassMutual and Mass General Brigham turned AI pilot sprawl into production results
Enterprise AI programs rarely fail because of bad ideas. More often, they get stuck in ungoverned pilot mode and never reach production. At a recent VentureBeat event, technology leaders from MassMutual and Mass General Brigham explained how they avoided that trap — and what the results look like when discipline replaces sprawl. At MassMutual, the results are concrete: 30% developer productivity gains, IT help desk resolution times reduced from 11 minutes to one, and customer service calls cut from 15 minutes to just one or two. “We're always starting with why do we care about this problem?” Sears Merritt, MassMutual’s head of enterprise technology and experience, said at the event. “If we solve the problem, how are we gonna know we solved it? And, how much value is associated with doing that?” MassMutual, a 175-year-old company serving millions of policy owners and customers, has pushed AI into production across the business — customer support, IT, customer acquisition, underwriting, servicing, claims, and other areas. Merritt said his team follows the scientific method, beginning with a hypothesis and testing whether it has an outcome that will tangibly drive the business forward. Some ideas are great, but they may be “intractable in the business” due to factors like lack of data or access, or regulatory constraint. “We won't go any further with an idea until we get crystal clear on how we're going to measure, and how we're going to define success.” Ultimately, it’s up to different departments and leaders to define what quality means: Choose a metric and define the minimum level of quality before a tool is placed into the hands of teams and partners. That starting point creates a quick feedback loop. “The things that we find slow us down is where there isn't shared clarity on what outcome we're trying to achieve,” which can lead to confusion and constant re-adjusting, said Merritt. “We don’t go to production until there is a business partner that says, ‘Yes, that works.’” His team is strategic about evaluating emerging tools, and “extremely rigorous” when testing and measuring what "good" means. For instance, they perform trust scoring to lower hallucination rates, establish thresholds and evaluation criteria, and monitor for feature and output drift. Merritt also operates with a no-commitment policy — meaning the company doesn’t lock itself into using a particular model. It has what he calls an “incredibly heterogeneous” technology environment combining best of breed models alongside mainframes running on COBOL. That flexibility isn't accidental. His team built common service layers, microservices and APIs that sit between the AI layer and everything underneath — so when a better model comes along, swapping it in doesn't mean starting over. Because, Merritt explained, “the best of breed today might be the worst of breed tomorrow, and we don't want to set ourselves up to fall behind.” Mass General Brigham (MGB), for its part, took more of a spray and pray approach — at first. Around 15,000 researchers in the not-for-profit health system have been using AI, ML, and deep learning for the last 10 to 15 years, CTO Nallan “Sri” Sriraman said at the same VB event. But last year, he made a bold choice: His team shut down a sprawl of non-governed AI pilots. Initially, “we did follow the thousand flowers bloom [methodology], but we didn't have a thousand flowers, we had probably a few tens of flowers trying to bloom,” he said. Like Merritt’s team at MassMutual, MGB pivoted to a more holistic view, examining why they were developing certain tools for specific departments of workflows. They questioned what capabilities they wanted and needed and what investment those required. Sriraman's team also spoke with their primary platform providers — Epic, Workday, ServiceNow, Microsoft — about their roadmaps. This was a “pivotal moment,” he noted, as they realized they were building in-house tools that vendors were already providing (or were planning to roll out). As Sriraman put it: “Why are we building it ourselves? We are already on the platform. It is going to be in the workflow. Leverage it.” That said, the marketplace is still nascent, which can make for difficult decisions. “The analogy I will give is when you ask six blind men to touch an elephant and say, what does this elephant look like?” Sriraman said. “You're gonna get six different answers.” There's nothing wrong with that, he noted; it's just that everybody is discovering and experimenting as the landscape keeps shifting. Instead of a wild West environment, Sriraman’s team distributes Microsoft Copilot to users across the business, and uses a “small landing zone” where they can safely test more sophisticated products and control token use. They also began “consciously embedding AI champions“ across business groups. “This is kind of a reverse of letting a thousand flowers bloom, carefully planting and nourishing,” Sriraman said. Observability is another big consideration; he describes real-time dashboards that manage model drift and safety and allow IT teams to govern AI “a little more pragmatically.” Health monitoring is critical with AI systems, he noted, and his team has established principles and policies around AI use, not to mention least access privileges. In clinical settings, the guardrails are absolute: AI systems never issue the final decision. "There's always going to be a doctor or a physician assistant in the loop to close the decision," Sriraman said. He cited radiology report generation as one area where AI is used heavily, but where a radiologist always signs off. Sriraman was clear: "Thou shall not do this: Don't show PHI [protected health information] in Perplexity. As simple as that, right?" And, importantly, there must be safety mechanisms in place. “We need a big red button, kill it,” Sriraman emphasized.

MassMutual和Mass General Brigham如何将人工智能试点扩张转化为生产成果
企业人工智能程序很少会因为糟糕的想法而失败。更常见的是，他们陷入了无人管理的试点模式，永远无法投入生产。在最近的VentureBeat活动中，MassMutual和Mass General Brigham的技术领导者解释了他们如何避免这种陷阱，以及当纪律取代蔓延时，结果会是什么样子。在万通人寿，结果是具体的：开发人员的生产力提高了30%，IT帮助台的解决时间从11分钟缩短到1分钟，客户服务电话从15分钟缩短到一两分钟。万通人寿企业技术和经验主管Sears Merritt在活动中表示：“我们总是从为什么要关心这个问题开始。”。“如果我们解决了这个问题，我们怎么知道我们解决了它？而且，这样做有多大价值？”MassMutual是一家拥有175年历史的公司，为数百万保单持有人和客户提供服务，它已经将人工智能推到了整个业务的生产中——客户支持、it、客户获取、承保、服务、索赔和其他领域。Merritt说，他的团队遵循科学方法，从假设开始，测试其结果是否会切实推动业务向前发展。有些想法很好，但由于缺乏数据或访问权限或监管限制等因素，它们可能“在业务中难以处理”。“在我们明确如何衡量以及如何定义成功之前，我们不会进一步提出一个想法。”最终，由不同的部门和领导来定义质量的含义：在将工具交给团队和合作伙伴之前，选择一个指标并定义最低质量水平。这个起点创造了一个快速的反馈循环。梅里特说：“我们发现，在我们试图实现的结果上没有共同的清晰度，这会导致混乱和不断的重新调整。”。“在有业务合作伙伴说‘是的，这很有效’之前，我们不会投入生产。”他的团队在评估新兴工具方面具有战略眼光，在测试和衡量“好”的含义时“非常严格”。例如，他们进行信任评分以降低幻觉率，建立阈值和评估标准，并监测特征和输出漂移。Merritt还实行无承诺政策，这意味着公司不会将自己锁定在使用特定模式上。它具有他所说的“令人难以置信的异构”技术环境，将一流的模型与运行在COBOL上的大型机相结合。这种灵活性并非偶然。他的团队构建了位于AI层和底层之间的公共服务层、微服务和API，因此当出现更好的模型时，交换它并不意味着重新开始。梅里特解释说，因为“今天最好的品种可能是明天最差的品种，我们不想让自己落后。”布里格姆将军（MGB）起初采取了更多的喷雾和祈祷方式。首席技术官Nallan“Sri”Sriraman在同一次VB活动上表示，在过去的10到15年里，非营利卫生系统中约有15000名研究人员一直在使用人工智能、机器学习和深度学习。但去年，他做出了一个大胆的选择：他的团队关闭了大量不受监管的人工智能飞行员。他说，最初，“我们确实遵循了千花盛开的方法，但我们没有一千朵花，我们可能有几十朵花试图盛开。”。与MassMutual的Merritt团队一样，MGB转向了更全面的视角，研究了他们为什么为特定的工作流程部门开发某些工具。他们质疑自己想要和需要什么能力，以及这些能力需要什么投资。Sriraman的团队还与他们的主要平台提供商——Epic、Workday、ServiceNow、微软——就他们的路线图进行了交谈。他指出，这是一个“关键时刻”，因为他们意识到他们正在构建供应商已经提供（或计划推出）的内部工具。正如Sriraman所说：“我们为什么要自己构建它？我们已经在平台上了。它将进入工作流程。利用它。”也就是说，市场仍然处于起步阶段，这可能会做出艰难的决定。斯里拉曼说：“我要做的类比是，当你让六个盲人触摸一头大象，然后说，这头大象长什么样？”。“你会得到六个不同的答案。”他指出，这没什么错；只是随着景观的不断变化，每个人都在发现和实验。Sriraman的团队没有在狂野的西部环境中，而是将Microsoft Copilot分发给整个企业的用户，并使用一个“小着陆区”，在那里他们可以安全地测试更复杂的产品并控制令牌的使用。他们还开始“有意识地将人工智能冠军嵌入”各个商业集团。“这有点像让一千朵花盛开，精心种植和滋养，”斯里拉曼说。可观察性是另一个重要考虑因素；他描述了管理模型漂移和安全性的实时仪表板，并允许IT团队“更务实地”管理人工智能。他指出，健康监测对人工智能系统至关重要，他的团队已经制定了关于人工智能使用的原则和政策，更不用说访问权限了。在临床环境中，护栏是绝对的：人工智能系统永远不会做出最终决定。斯里拉曼说：“总是会有一名医生或助理医生来完成决定。”。他指出，放射学报告生成是人工智能被大量使用的一个领域，但放射科医生总是会在其中签字。斯里拉曼明确表示：“你不应该这样做：不要在困惑中显示PHI（受保护的健康信息）。就这么简单，对吧？”而且，重要的是，必须有安全机制。斯里拉曼强调：“我们需要一个红色的大按钮，把它干掉。”。
