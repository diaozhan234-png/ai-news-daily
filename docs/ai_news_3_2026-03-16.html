Fixing AI failure: Three changes enterprises should make now
Recent reports about AI project failure rates have raised uncomfortable questions for organizations investing heavily in AI. Much of the discussion has focused on technical factors like model accuracy and data quality, but after watching dozens of AI initiatives launch, I’ve noticed that the biggest opportunities for improvement are often cultural, not technical. Internal projects that struggle tend to share common issues. For example, engineering teams build models that product managers don’t know how to use. Data scientists build prototypes that operations teams struggle to maintain. And AI applications sit unused because the people they were built for weren't involved in deciding what “useful” really meant. In contrast, organizations that achieve meaningful value with AI have figured out how to create the right kind of collaboration across departments, and established shared accountability for outcomes. The technology matters, but the organizational readiness matters just as much. Here are three practices I’ve observed that address the cultural and organizational barriers that can impede AI success. When only engineers understand how an AI system works and what it’s capable of, collaboration breaks down. Product managers can't evaluate trade-offs they don't understand. Designers can't create interfaces for capabilities they can't articulate. Analysts can't validate outputs they can't interpret. The solution isn't making everyone a data scientist. It's helping each role understand how AI applies to their specific work. Product managers need to grasp what kinds of generated content, predictions or recommendations are realistic given available data. Designers need to understand what the AI can actually do so they can design features users will find useful. Analysts need to know which AI outputs require human validation versus which can be trusted. When teams share this working vocabulary, AI stops being something that happens in the engineering department and becomes a tool the entire organization can use effectively. The second challenge involves knowing where AI can act on its own versus where human approval is required. Many organizations default to extremes, either bottlenecking every AI decision through human review, or letting AI systems operate without guardrails . What's needed is a clear framework that defines where and how AI can act autonomously. This means establishing rules upfront: Can AI approve routine configuration changes? Can it recommend schema updates but not implement them? Can it deploy code to staging environments but not production? These rules should include three elements: auditability (can you trace how the AI reached its decision?), reproducibility (can you recreate the decision path?), and observability (can teams monitor AI behavior as it happens?). Without this framework, you either slow down to the point where AI provides no advantage, or you create systems making decisions nobody can explain or control. The third step is codifying how different teams actually work with AI systems. When every department develops its own approach, you get inconsistent results and redundant effort. Cross-functional playbooks work best when teams develop them together rather than having them imposed from above. These playbooks answer concrete questions like: How do we test AI recommendations before putting them into production? What's our fallback procedure when an automated deployment fails – does it hand off to human operators or try a different approach first? Who needs to be involved when we override an AI decision? How do we incorporate feedback to improve the system? The goal isn't to add bureaucracy. It's ensuring everyone understands how AI fits into their existing work, and what to do when results don't match expectations. Technical excellence in AI remains important, but enterprises that over-index on model performance while ignoring organizational factors are setting themselves up for avoidable challenges. The successful AI deployments I’ve seen treat cultural transformation and workflows just as seriously as technical implementation. The question isn't whether your AI technology is sophisticated enough. It's whether your organization is ready to work with it. 修复人工智能失败：企业现在应该做出的三个改变
最近关于人工智能项目失败率的报道给大量投资人工智能的组织提出了令人不安的问题。大部分讨论都集中在模型准确性和数据质量等技术因素上，但在观看了数十项人工智能项目的启动后，我注意到最大的改进机会往往是文化上的，而不是技术上的。有困难的内部项目往往有共同的问题。例如，工程团队构建了产品经理不知道如何使用的模型。数据科学家构建了运营团队难以维护的原型。人工智能应用程序没有被使用，因为它们为之构建的人没有参与决定“有用”的真正含义。相比之下，利用人工智能实现有意义价值的组织已经找到了如何在各部门之间建立正确的协作，并建立了对结果的共同问责制。技术很重要，但组织的准备程度同样重要。以下是我观察到的三种做法，可以解决阻碍人工智能成功的文化和组织障碍。当只有工程师了解人工智能系统的工作原理及其功能时，协作就会崩溃。产品经理无法评估他们不理解的权衡。设计师无法为他们无法表达的功能创建界面。分析师无法验证他们无法解释的输出。解决方案不是让每个人都成为数据科学家。它帮助每个角色了解人工智能如何应用于他们的特定工作。产品经理需要掌握在现有数据的情况下，哪些类型的生成内容、预测或建议是现实的。设计师需要了解人工智能实际上能做什么，这样他们才能设计出用户会觉得有用的功能。分析师需要知道哪些AI输出需要人工验证，哪些可以信任。当团队共享这些工作词汇时，人工智能就不再是工程部门发生的事情，而是成为整个组织可以有效使用的工具。第二个挑战涉及了解人工智能在哪些方面可以独立行动，而在哪些方面需要人类的批准。许多组织默认了极端情况，要么通过人工审查来限制每一个人工智能决策，要么让人工智能系统在没有护栏的情况下运行。我们需要的是一个明确的框架，定义人工智能可以在哪里以及如何自主行动。这意味着要预先制定规则：人工智能可以批准常规配置更改吗？它能推荐模式更新但不实施吗？它可以将代码部署到临时环境，但不能部署到生产环境吗？这些规则应该包括三个要素：可审计性（你能追踪人工智能是如何做出决定的吗？）、可重复性（你可以重新创建决策路径吗？）和可观察性（团队可以在人工智能行为发生时进行监控吗？）。如果没有这个框架，你要么放慢速度，让人工智能没有优势，要么你创建的系统做出没有人能解释或控制的决策。第三步是编纂不同团队如何实际使用人工智能系统。当每个部门都制定自己的方法时，你会得到不一致的结果和多余的努力。当团队共同开发跨职能剧本时，而不是自上而下强加时，跨职能剧本最有效。这些剧本回答了具体的问题，比如：在投入生产之前，我们如何测试人工智能建议？当自动化部署失败时，我们的后备程序是什么？它是交给人工操作员还是先尝试不同的方法？当我们推翻人工智能的决定时，谁需要参与？我们如何整合反馈以改进系统？我们的目标不是增加官僚主义。它确保每个人都了解人工智能如何融入他们现有的工作，以及当结果不符合预期时该怎么办。人工智能的技术卓越仍然很重要，但那些过度关注模型性能而忽视组织因素的企业正在为本可避免的挑战做好准备。我所看到的成功的人工智能部署将文化转型和工作流程与技术实施同等重视。问题不在于你的人工智能技术是否足够复杂。这取决于你的组织是否准备好与之合作。Adi Polak是Confluent的宣传和开发人员体验工程总监。欢迎来到VentureBeat社区！我们的客座项目是技术专家分享见解，并就人工智能、数据基础设施、网络安全和其他塑造企业未来的尖端技术提供中立、非既定的深入研究。请阅读我们的客座文章计划中的更多内容，如果您有兴趣撰写自己的文章，请查看我们的指南！
