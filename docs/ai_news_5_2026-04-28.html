Microsoft and OpenAI gut their exclusive deal, freeing OpenAI to sell on AWS and Google Cloud
Microsoft and OpenAI on Monday announced a sweeping overhaul of the partnership that has defined the commercial AI era, dismantling key pillars of exclusivity and revenue-sharing that bound the two companies together for years and replacing them with a looser, time-limited arrangement that gives both sides far more freedom to pursue rival relationships. The amended agreement, disclosed simultaneously in blog posts from both companies , marks the most significant restructuring since Microsoft first invested $1 billion in OpenAI in 2019 — and it transforms what was once the most consequential exclusive technology alliance in a generation into something that more closely resembles a strategic but arm's-length commercial relationship. Under the new terms, Microsoft will no longer pay any revenue share to OpenAI when customers access OpenAI models through Azure. OpenAI, meanwhile, will continue paying a revenue share to Microsoft through 2030 — at the same 20 percent rate — but that obligation is now subject to a total cap . Microsoft retains a license to OpenAI's intellectual property for models and products through 2032, but that license is now explicitly non-exclusive. And OpenAI, critically, can now serve all of its products to customers on any cloud provider — including Amazon Web Services and Google Cloud — ending the exclusivity that had been a cornerstone of the original deal. "The rapid pace of innovation requires us to continue to evolve our partnership to benefit our customers and both companies," Microsoft wrote in its blog post Monday. OpenAI echoed the framing, calling the amended agreement a move "grounded in flexibility, certainty, and a focus on delivering the benefits of AI broadly." The diplomatic language belies the drama that led to this moment — months of behind-the-scenes tension, competing deal announcements, public contradictions, and even the specter of litigation between two companies whose fates have been intertwined since the earliest days of the generative AI revolution. To understand why Monday's announcement matters so much, it helps to understand what came before it. When Microsoft poured its initial $1 billion into OpenAI in 2019 , and then followed with a cumulative investment exceeding $13 billion , it secured something extraordinary: exclusive commercial access to OpenAI's models and intellectual property. Azure became the sole cloud provider for OpenAI's API products . Microsoft integrated OpenAI's GPT models into everything from Bing to Office to GitHub Copilot. The arrangement was, by any measure, one of the most lopsided technology licensing deals in modern history — Microsoft got privileged access to the most capable AI models on the planet, and OpenAI got the capital and infrastructure it needed to scale. The deal even contained an unusual provision: Microsoft's exclusive rights would remain in force until OpenAI achieved artificial general intelligence , or AGI — a loosely defined milestone referring to AI systems that rival or exceed human intelligence across a broad range of tasks. OpenAI's board retained the authority to declare when AGI had been reached, at which point certain commercial terms would change. It was, in effect, a philosophical tripwire embedded in a business contract. That structure worked well enough when OpenAI was a research lab with a modest commercial footprint. But as ChatGPT exploded into the mainstream in late 2022 and OpenAI's annualized revenue rocketed into the billions , the constraints began to chafe. OpenAI found itself locked into a single cloud ecosystem at precisely the moment when enterprises — its fastest-growing customer segment — were demanding multi-cloud flexibility. In an internal memo earlier this month, OpenAI's revenue chief Denise Dresser put it bluntly, telling staff that the Microsoft partnership had " limited our ability to meet enterprises where they are ," according to a report from The Verge. The proximate cause of Monday's restructuring was not a philosophical disagreement about AI safety or corporate governance. It was a $50 billion check from Amazon. In February, OpenAI announced that Amazon would invest up to $50 billion in the company — $15 billion upfront, with another $35 billion to follow when certain unspecified conditions were met. In exchange, OpenAI agreed to expand its existing cloud agreement with AWS by $100 billion over eight years and, most controversially, committed to making AWS the exclusive third-party distribution provider for Frontier , its new enterprise agent-building platform. OpenAI also agreed to co-develop "stateful runtime technology" on AWS Bedrock, the infrastructure layer that allows AI agents to maintain memory and context over extended tasks. The problem was that OpenAI's existing contract with Microsoft almost certainly prohibited these arrangements. Microsoft held exclusive rights to any OpenAI product accessed through an API — a category that plainly included Frontier. On the very day OpenAI announced the Amazon deal, Microsoft issued a pointed public statement insisting that "Azure remains the exclusive cloud provider of stateless OpenAI APIs" and that "OpenAI's first party products, including Frontier, will continue to be hosted on Azure." The contradiction between the two announcements was stark, and it created immediate legal exposure. The Financial Times reported in March that Microsoft was actively considering legal action to enforce its contractual rights. The situation placed OpenAI in an impossible position: it had made promises to Amazon that it seemingly could not keep under the terms of its Microsoft agreement. Monday's deal resolves that impasse entirely. By converting Microsoft's license from exclusive to non-exclusive and explicitly granting OpenAI the right to serve products on any cloud, the new terms retroactively validate the Amazon arrangement and eliminate the legal overhang. Amazon CEO Andy Jassy wasted no time celebrating.

微软和OpenAI取消了独家协议，使OpenAI可以在AWS和谷歌云上销售
微软和OpenAI周一宣布对定义了商业人工智能时代的合作关系进行全面改革，拆除了将两家公司联系在一起多年的排他性和收入共享的关键支柱，取而代之的是更宽松、有时限的安排，让双方有更大的自由来追求竞争关系。这项修订后的协议同时在两家公司的博客文章中披露，标志着自2019年微软首次向OpenAI投资10亿美元以来最重大的重组，它将曾经是一代人中最重要的独家技术联盟转变为更接近战略但保持距离的商业关系。根据新条款，当客户通过Azure访问OpenAI模型时，微软将不再向OpenAI支付任何收入分成。与此同时，OpenAI将在2030年之前继续向微软支付收入份额，比例为20%，但这一义务现在受到总上限的限制。微软在2032年之前保留了OpenAI模型和产品知识产权的许可证，但该许可证现在是明确的非排他性的。至关重要的是，OpenAI现在可以向任何云提供商（包括亚马逊网络服务和谷歌云）的客户提供其所有产品，从而结束了作为原始交易基石的排他性。微软周一在其博客中写道：“快速的创新步伐要求我们继续发展我们的合作伙伴关系，以造福我们的客户和两家公司。”。OpenAI呼应了这一框架，称修订后的协议是“基于灵活性、确定性和对广泛提供人工智能好处的关注”。外交语言掩盖了导致这一时刻的戏剧性——数月的幕后紧张局势、相互竞争的交易公告、公开矛盾，甚至两家公司之间的诉讼幽灵，这两家公司的命运自生成人工智能革命的早期以来就交织在一起。要理解为什么周一的公告如此重要，有助于理解之前的情况。当微软在2019年向OpenAI投入最初的10亿美元，然后累计投资超过130亿美元时，它获得了一些非同寻常的东西：对OpenAI模型和知识产权的独家商业访问权。Azure成为OpenAI API产品的唯一云提供商。微软将OpenAI的GPT模型集成到从Bing到Office再到GitHub Copilot的所有内容中。无论如何，这一安排都是现代历史上最不平衡的技术许可协议之一——微软获得了访问地球上最强大的人工智能模型的特权，OpenAI获得了扩展所需的资本和基础设施。该协议甚至包含了一项不同寻常的条款：在OpenAI实现通用人工智能（AGI）之前，微软的独家权利将一直有效。AGI是一个定义松散的里程碑，指的是在各种任务中与人类智能相匹敌或超越人类智能的人工智能系统。OpenAI董事会保留了宣布何时达到AGI的权力，届时某些商业条款将发生变化。实际上，这是一个嵌入商业合同中的哲学绊网。当OpenAI还是一个商业足迹适中的研究实验室时，这种结构运作得很好。但随着ChatGPT在2022年底迅速成为主流，OpenAI的年收入飙升至数十亿美元，这些限制开始受到质疑。OpenAI发现自己被锁定在一个单一的云生态系统中，而此时企业——其增长最快的客户群体——正在要求多云灵活性。根据the Verge的一份报告，在本月早些时候的一份内部备忘录中，OpenAI的营收主管Denise Dresser直言不讳地告诉员工，与微软的合作“限制了我们与现有企业会面的能力”。周一重组的直接原因并不是对人工智能安全或公司治理的哲学分歧。这是一张来自亚马逊的500亿美元支票。今年2月，OpenAI宣布亚马逊将向该公司投资高达500亿美元，其中150亿美元为预付款，当满足某些未指明的条件时，还将再投资350亿美元。作为交换，OpenAI同意在八年内将其与AWS的现有云协议扩大1000亿美元，并且最具争议的是，致力于使AWS成为其新的企业代理构建平台Frontier的独家第三方分销提供商。OpenAI还同意在AWS Bedrock上共同开发“有状态运行时技术”，该基础设施层允许AI代理在扩展任务上维护内存和上下文。问题是OpenAI与微软的现有合同几乎肯定禁止这些安排。微软拥有通过API访问任何OpenAI产品的独家权利，这一类别显然包括Frontier。就在OpenAI宣布与亚马逊达成协议的当天，微软发表了一份尖锐的公开声明，坚称“Azure仍然是无状态OpenAI API的独家云提供商”，并且“OpenAI的第一方产品，包括Frontier，将继续托管在Azure上。”这两个公告之间的矛盾是显而易见的，它立即造成了法律风险。英国《金融时报》3月份报道称，微软正在积极考虑采取法律行动来执行其合同权利。这种情况使OpenAI陷入了一个不可能的境地：它向亚马逊做出了承诺，但似乎无法遵守其与微软的协议条款。周一的协议彻底打破了僵局。通过将微软的许可证从独家转换为非独家，并明确授予OpenAI在任何云上提供产品的权利，新条款追溯性地验证了亚马逊的安排，并消除了法律上的悬置。亚马逊首席执行官Andy Jassy没有浪费时间庆祝。
