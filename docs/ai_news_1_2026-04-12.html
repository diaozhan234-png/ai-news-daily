Your article about AI doesn’t need AI art
﻿A New Yorker profile of Sam Altman was accompanied by controversial artwork. The illustration for The New Yorker ’s profile of OpenAI CEO Sam Altman is a jump scare. Altman stands in a blue sweater with a blank expression. Around his head hovers a cluster of disembodied faces — creepy alt-Altmans, their expressions ranging from anger to open-mouthed woe. Some barely look like Altman. One final face rests in his hands. And at the bottom, there’s a disclosure that might spook many illustrators far more: “Visual by David Szauder; Generated using A.I.” Szauder is a mixed-media artist who has been working with collage, video, and generative art processes that predate commercial AI tools for over a decade, and was recently teaching art and technology at Moholy-Nagy University of Art and Design in Budapest. Here, his work leans into the shifty uncanniness of Altman’s two (or more)-facedness. The pained-looking expressions on the faces and a gloss of eerie motion smoothing communicate the central thesis that Altman can’t be trusted. There’s a painterly look to the image, rather than the typical slop-style sickly sheen, but the AI origins are still unmistakable. What does it say for The New Yorker , one of America’s most prestigious magazines, to adopt generative AI? At its worst, the technology eliminates any discernable art process, flattening the creator’s intention — it’s a system for making pregnant videos of LeBron James and Italian Brainrot , not creations that rival the work of New Yorker illustrators like Kadir Nelson , Christoph Niemann , or Victo Ngai . In Szauder’s hands, it’s far more complicated: one piece of a longer creative process, which apparently includes programming his own AI tools and feeding them archival imagery, like newspaper clippings and family photos. Yet it’s still, in my opinion, a waste of an opportunity. Human artists have designed creative parodies of AI slop , but AI lacks the necessary self-awareness to parody itself, even with a human behind the wheel. The image relies on the unsettling nature of AI animation to tell its story without really saying anything new about AI imagery or the industry behind it all. When we reached out to Szauder, while he wasn’t specific about which AI tools he used, he did explain the process of the piece in some detail. There is usually a sketch stage prior to delivering any final imagery. The New Yorker ’s digital design director, Aviva Michaelov, says that Szauder sent around 15 different sketches to senior art director Supriya Kalidas, including the one that eventually led to the final Hydra-esque eldritch monstrosity that can be seen above the article. In an email to us, Szauder writes: “For the base structure of the final image, I had a clear idea of how I wanted to position the character and its heads. So AI functioned even more as a tool than usual, especially since much of the work focused on shaping the faces, the heads, the portraits, through a combination of classical editing methods (Photoshop, if we want to name it) and AI-based editing. The results were often imperfect or flawed, which required manual correction and refinement. We spent considerable time refining facial expressions, while also developing multiple variations in clothing and repeatedly adjusting the lighting to arrive at the final image.” According to a 2025 article on Szauder from Whitehot Magazine , he “managed to devise his own coding system and programming software to generate images based on a particular prompt or archival image materials he feeds into its design.” He also seems concerned with the moral quandary of traditional AI image generation, using “ethically clarified source materials.” As Szauder explained to us, “I strongly believe that even in the age of AI, an image must first be formed in the human mind, not in the machine.” This is a far deeper human touch than goes into much AI-generated work. The ensloppification of newsrooms has been well documented by other Verge writers . Great journalists across the industry have been completely replaced by AI or told that, to keep their jobs, they have no choice but to find ways to use it. The topic ( and controversies ) of AI use in illustration is reliably a cortisol spike for most illustrators. It’s not the first time a renowned publication has dabbled in AI . It’s also not the first time The New Yorker has commissioned David Szauder to create an AI animated illustration . Here at The Verge , we hold a strict policy on the use of AI-generated imagery. We slap a yellow label on any image we publish that’s been generated with AI, and any time we use AI image generation to assist with the creation of an image it is disclosed, loudly, and with clear justification . (Disclosure: Our parent company, Vox Media, has an agreement with OpenAI .) In many cases, generated images — particularly those created purely through text prompts, likely the most common method — strip out the creation process that makes art human. The input from a text field only has so much effect on the output, to the point that AI-generated images created this way can’t be copyrighted. According to a guidance from the US Copyright Office on the legal authorship of AI-generated images , “No matter how many times a prompt is revised and resubmitted, the final output reflects the user’s acceptance of the AI system’s interpretation, rather than authorship of the expression it contains.” The eye of an artist is informed by a lifetime of assembling an internal library of taste, meaning, and intent, none of which are possessed by tools like Midjourney or ChatGPT. The results of image prompts often feel like somebody describing a dream: It’s fascinating when your brain assembles it, but tell another person your surrealist vision about making out with your therapist before all of your teeth turned to dust and disintegrated, and their eyes glaze over until the subject changes back to the weather.

你关于人工智能的文章不需要人工智能艺术
﻿《纽约客》对Sam Altman的简介伴随着有争议的艺术品。奥特曼站在一件蓝色毛衣里，面无表情。在他的头上盘旋着一群没有实体的面孔——令人毛骨悚然的Altmans，他们的表情从愤怒到张大嘴巴的悲伤。有些人看起来几乎不像奥特曼。最后一张脸落在他的手中。在底部，有一项披露可能会让许多插画家更加害怕：“视觉由David Szauder创作；使用人工智能生成。”Szaude是一位混合媒体艺术家，十多年来一直在研究拼贴、视频和生成艺术过程，这些过程早于商业人工智能工具，最近在布达佩斯的Moholy-Nagy艺术与设计大学教授艺术和技术。在这里，他的作品倾向于奥特曼两张（或更多）脸的鬼鬼祟祟。脸上痛苦的表情和怪异的运动平滑光泽传达了奥特曼不可信的中心论点。这张照片有一种绘画般的外观，而不是典型的邋遢风格病态的光泽，但人工智能的起源仍然是毋庸置疑的。对于美国最负盛名的杂志之一《纽约客》采用生成式人工智能有什么看法？在最糟糕的情况下，这项技术消除了任何可辨别的艺术过程，淡化了创作者的意图——这是一个制作勒布朗·詹姆斯和意大利人布伦洛特怀孕视频的系统，而不是与卡迪尔·纳尔逊、克里斯托夫·尼曼或维克托·恩盖等纽约插画家的作品相匹敌的作品。在Szauder的手中，情况要复杂得多：这是一个更长的创作过程的一部分，显然包括为自己的人工智能工具编程，并向它们提供档案图像，如剪报和家庭照片。然而，在我看来，这仍然是浪费机会。人类艺术家设计了对人工智能垃圾的创造性模仿，但人工智能缺乏必要的自我意识来模仿自己，即使有人在开车。这张图片依赖于人工智能动画令人不安的性质来讲述它的故事，而没有真正谈论人工智能图像或其背后的行业。当我们联系Szauder时，虽然他没有具体说明他使用了哪些人工智能工具，但他确实详细解释了这篇文章的过程。在交付任何最终图像之前，通常有一个草图阶段。《纽约客》的数字设计总监Aviva Michaelov表示，Szouder向高级艺术总监Supriya Kalidas发送了大约15幅不同的草图，其中一幅最终导致了Hydra风格的eldritch怪物，可以在文章上方看到。在给我们的一封电子邮件中，Szauder写道：“对于最终图像的基本结构，我清楚地知道我想如何定位角色及其头部。因此，人工智能比平时更像是一种工具，特别是因为大部分工作都集中在通过经典编辑方法（如果我们想命名的话，可以称之为Photoshop）和基于人工智能的编辑来塑造面部、头部和肖像。结果往往不完美或有缺陷，需要手动校正和改进。我们花了相当多的时间来改善面部表情，同时还开发了多种服装变体，并反复调整照明以获得最终图像。根据《Whitehot Magazine》2025年关于Szauder的一篇文章，他“设法设计了自己的编码系统和编程软件，根据他输入到设计中的特定提示或档案图像材料生成图像。”他似乎也担心传统人工智能图像生成的道德困境，使用“道德澄清的源材料”。正如Szouder向我们解释的那样，“我坚信，即使在人工智能时代，图像也必须首先在人类大脑中形成，而不是在机器中形成。”这是一种比许多人工智能生成的工作更深刻的人性化。新闻编辑室的本土化已经被其他Verge作家很好地记录下来。整个行业的优秀记者已经被人工智能完全取代，或者被告知，为了保住工作，他们别无选择，只能找到使用人工智能的方法。对于大多数插画家来说，人工智能在插画中的使用（和争议）无疑是一个皮质醇激增的话题。这不是第一次有知名出版物涉足人工智能。这也不是《纽约客》第一次委托大卫·绍德创作人工智能动画插图。在The Verge，我们对使用人工智能生成的图像有严格的政策。我们在发布的任何由人工智能生成的图像上贴上黄色标签，任何时候我们使用人工智能图像生成来协助创建图像时，都会大声地、明确地披露出来。（披露：我们的母公司Vox Media与OpenAI签订了协议。）在许多情况下，生成的图像——尤其是那些纯粹通过文本提示创建的图像，这可能是最常见的方法——剥夺了使艺术人性化的创作过程。文本字段的输入对输出的影响有限，以至于以这种方式创建的人工智能生成的图像不能受到版权保护。根据美国版权局关于人工智能生成图像的合法作者身份的指导，“无论提示被修改和重新提交多少次，最终输出都反映了用户对人工智能系统解释的接受程度，而不是它所包含表达的作者身份。”艺术家的眼睛是通过一生组装一个包含品味、意义和意图的内部库来感知的，而Midjourney或ChatGPT等工具都没有这些库。图像提示的结果通常感觉就像有人在描述一个梦：当你的大脑组装它时，这很有趣，但告诉另一个人你的超现实主义愿景，即在你所有的牙齿都变成灰尘和碎裂之前，与你的治疗师亲热，他们的眼睛变得呆滞，直到受试者变回天气。
